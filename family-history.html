From Ulster to Rocky Spring: The Story of John Wead

Scots‑Irish Roots and a New Life in Pennsylvania

Born 1710 in County Cavan, Ireland, John Wead (Wade) was part of the Scots‑Irish Presbyterian migration that brought determined Ulster families to colonial America. He arrived in Pennsylvania between 1728 and 1730. This date range is drawn from the timing of his 1738 land warrant and aligns with the larger wave of Ulster Scots immigration into the region during those years. John followed a familiar migration path through Chester and Lancaster before pushing west into the unsettled Cumberland Valley. In 1738, he secured a Pennsylvania land warrant for 200 acres near the confluence of Falling Spring and the Conococheague—then a rugged frontier zone. His tract lay just north of Benjamin Chambers’s mill and bordered the properties of early neighbors like William Kelso and James Gay. By carving out a homestead there, John stood among the earliest settlers of what would become Letterkenny Township. He helped build both farm and fellowship, anchoring a Scots-Irish community that would thrive for generations.

When the French & Indian War (1754–63) spilled into the Cumberland Valley, the Wade homestead sat on the very line of danger. In November 1755, Shawnee and Lenape warriors struck the settlements west of Rocky Spring in the Great Cove massacre—burning cabins only a day’s ride from John Wade’s farm and carrying off dozens of neighbors as prisoners. Raids continued down the Conococheague frontier through 1756, forcing many families to abandon their claims and crowd into makeshift stockades; Rocky Spring itself was fortified with a log “fort meeting-house” during the crisis.

In this climate of panic—rifle shots at night, alarms from Fort Loudoun, and rumors that even church elders were taken captive—John Wade enlisted in the Pennsylvania Provincial Troops. Muster rolls place him on active duty in 1758‑59, the years of Forbes’s march toward Fort Duquesne. Whether he was ever wounded or briefly captured is unknown, but his own 1789 will later claims “the arrears of pay due me for the Expedition,” confirming both service and long‑overdue wages.

The threat was so acute that in February 1760—with militia patrols thin and rumors of a renewed assault—John drafted his first will. It conveyed his “plantation near Rocky Spring” to his seventeen‑year‑old son Ebenezer and provided cash legacies and livestock for daughters Fanny, Elizabeth, and Mary. He also ordered that his musket, rifle, and powder horn were never to be sold outside the family, a sober reminder that firearms were both livelihood and lifeline on the far frontier. In a single document we glimpse a father’s resolve to secure his children’s future, even as war, disease, and displacement stalked the Falling Spring settlement.

Homestead Legacy: From Warrant to Community

John's decision to prepare his estate bore fruit. In 1765, his son Ebenezer received a formal land warrant from the Proprietary government, confirming improvements made decades earlier by "John Waid, father of said Ebenezer." This wasn’t just a legal formality—it recognized the family's right to nearly 480 acres shaped by John’s early labor. The Wead homestead encompassed fields, woodlots, and springs in an area that became a hub of Scots-Irish settlement. It stood near the Rocky Spring Presbyterian meetinghouse and bordered farms owned by the Culbertsons, Andersons, and Boyds—families tied together by marriage, kinship, and shared faith. John’s choice of land and his long residence there reflected not only persistence but quiet leadership. His presence helped transform a contested edge of empire into a rooted community.

Later Years, Faith, and Family

John Wead remained on the land for the rest of his life. In tax rolls from the 1780s, he appears as a stable landholder with domestic help, suggesting ongoing productivity and community standing. His faith never wavered. When Rocky Spring Presbyterian Church formally organized in the 1780s, John was already a regular at the log meeting house. An inventory after his death revealed a personal library of theological works—Westminster Confession, volumes of sermons, and Robert Wodrow’s History of the Sufferings of the Church of Scotland—showing both his literacy and deep Presbyterian conviction. In 1789, just months before his death, he drafted a new will. This final testament provided for his wife Janet (née Gabby), left gifts to his grandson John McConnell and nephew William Gabby, and notably remembered his brother William Wead of Bailieborough, County Cavan—a rare and precious transatlantic genealogical link.

A Well-Provisioned Man of Principle

When John Wead died in 1790, he left behind more than 200 acres of improved land, a herd of livestock, and a home well-stocked with tools, furniture, and religious books. His personal inventory included nearly two dozen cows and heifers, over 100 sheep, and a wagon, suggesting he ran a productive, self-sufficient farm. Yet nothing in his estate was extravagant—every item, from a dung fork to a family Bible, reflected utility and conviction.

His library was steeped in theology and Scottish history. Titles like The Confession of Faith and Wodrow’s History of the Church of Scotland reveal a man who lived—and taught—his Presbyterian values. He was literate, devout, and trusted by his community. His wife Janet, three decades his junior, was not only his final spouse but one of two executors of his estate. The other? His neighbor and friend James McConnell.

This was no solitary farmer. This was a man respected by neighbors, remembered in Ireland, and loved by family.

From Pennsylvania to the Western Frontier: A Lasting Legacy

John Wead died on January 17, 1790, at the age of 79, and was buried in the Rocky Spring cemetery he helped settle more than 50 years earlier. But his story didn’t end there. In the following decade, his descendants joined the wave of westward migration. Ebenezer Wead and his family moved to Kentucky in the 1790s and later crossed into the Ohio frontier, establishing roots near Dayton. There, the Weads built new homes, founded churches, and petitioned authorities for security as the young republic expanded. Ebenezer lived until 1830, bridging the colonial frontier and early Ohio statehood. Through each chapter, John’s descendants carried his values—faith, hard work, land stewardship, and readiness to defend home.

A musket once held on a rocky Pennsylvania hillside may have ridden in a wagon across the Alleghenies, a silent heirloom of courage and conviction. Today, John Wead’s legacy endures as a powerful emblem of the Scots-Irish American experience—a tale of grit, grace, and generational endurance.
